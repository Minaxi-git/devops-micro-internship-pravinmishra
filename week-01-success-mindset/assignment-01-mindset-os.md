The Program Manager Who Learned to Build What She Used to Govern
Minaxi Punjabi

A profile, five years out*

When Minaxi Punjabi talks about the shift from managing AI programs to building AI systems herself, she doesn’t reach for a big turning-point story. She reaches for a spreadsheet.

“I had spent two decades writing the requirements other people coded to,” she says. “At some point I got tired of translating. I wanted to know what I was translating.”

Most people call this reinvention. It’s closer to quality control. Punjabi was already a name in federal IT circles: NSF, the Federal Reserve Board, CMS, SEC, USDA, HHS — twenty-plus years of program delivery in agencies where a missed milestone shows up as eroded public trust, not just a slipped budget line. She knew how to run an Agile Release Train, and she knew how a governance framework catches a failure in week two or lets it compound quietly for eighteen months. What she hadn’t done was build the thing the framework was written for. So she closed that gap on her own terms, before anyone else had reason to point it out.

Become a Medium member
So she went and learned it — Terraform, Kubernetes, CI/CD pipelines, agentic AI architecture — the least glamorous decision available to someone at her level: sit in a cohort with people half her tenure, and be a beginner on purpose.

The result isn’t a career pivot. It’s a credibility upgrade. She still does what she always did — dependency mapping, risk frameworks, the unsexy work of keeping large federal AI programs from quietly falling apart. The difference: when an engineer calls a constraint technical, she can now tell whether that’s true or whether it’s a negotiating position. That’s the whole signature. She governs AI systems the way you govern something you could have built yourself.

The advice she gives most often to program managers eyeing an AI-adjacent role is short: the gap is smaller than it looks. Most of it closes the day you stop being impressed by the technology and start being annoyed that you don’t understand it.
